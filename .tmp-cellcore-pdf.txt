1. Executive Summary
This case study explains how I designed an AI-assisted machine learning and analytics system to 
model product purchase sequences, reorder likelihood, protocol adherence, and next-best-product 
recommendations for a protocol-based wellness commerce business. The project context was CellCore 
Biosciences / Microbe Formulas, a professional supplement and protocol brand with both practitioner 
and consumer-facing commerce dynamics.
The core insight was that ordinary reorder analytics understated true customer momentum. Reorder 
likelihood was generally low when customers purchased isolated products, but it increased materially 
when the customer was attached to a practitioner, protocol, pathway, bundle, or known phase 
sequence. Standard ecommerce reporting could count repeat orders, but it could not explain 
sequence behavior: what customers did next, where they exited, which products pulled them deeper 
into a protocol, and which cohorts behaved like one-time buyers versus structured pathway 
followers.
I designed the system as one broader ML project with multiple modeling layers rather than a 
collection of unrelated projects. The centerpiece was a Markov chain product prediction model. 
Supporting layers included higher-order sequence modeling, reorder propensity scoring, market 
basket association rules, behavioral customer segmentation, and a weighted ranking layer that 
converted model outputs into practical next-product recommendations.

simply, 'Who bought again?' It is, 'Which pathway does this purchase imply, and what should happen

3. Problem Scenario
The practical scenario was a product and customer journey problem hiding inside ordinary order 
history. Leadership and commercial teams needed to know whether customers were likely to reorder 
the same product, continue into another product, enter a protocol sequence, or disappear after an 
initial purchase. The dataset existed in business systems, but the insight was not available from 
normal dashboards because the problem required sequence reconstruction.

4. Why the Problem Was Hard
The hard part was not choosing a trendy model. The hard part was turning messy commerce data into 
a sequence-safe, privacy-safe, business-readable model. A simple neural recommender would have 
been harder to explain, harder to govern, more fragile with sparse protocol behavior, and less useful 
to non-technical stakeholders. The appropriate solution was a transparent probabilistic sequence 
model supported by carefully engineered features and validation loops.

6.4 Sequence construction logic
The sequence builder avoided a common modeling mistake: treating same-order line items as ordered 
events. Within one order, product line order is often arbitrary. The safer design was to model same-
order items as a basket for affinity and order-to-order changes as sequence transitions.
1. 1. Filter to valid paid or fulfilled orders.
2. 2. Normalize each SKU to a durable product key and map it to category/protocol metadata.
3. 3. Group all line items in the same order into a basket state.
4. 4. Sort each customer's baskets by timestamp.
5. 5. Emit first-order transitions: basket_t -> basket_t+1 or state_t -> state_t+1.
6. 6. Emit higher-order contexts when at least two prior states exist.
7. 7. Partition counts by cohort: practitioner-led, direct consumer, protocol, bundle exposure, first-time buyer, 
subscription, and channel.
7. Model Stack and Methods
The model stack was deliberately composable. Each layer answered a different question, produced a 
reusable output, and fed the final ranking layer. This made the system easier to debug, explain, and 
improve without rewriting the entire architecture.
7.1 Layer 1: First-order Markov chain
Purpose: predict the next likely product, product family, basket, or protocol state from the customer's 
most recent state.
Formula: P(next_state = j | current_state = i) = (C_ij + alpha) / (sum_k C_ik + alpha * K)
Where C_ij is the number of observed transitions from state i to state j, alpha is the smoothing 
parameter, and K is the number of possible next states. Smoothing prevents zero-probability 
recommendations for sparse but plausible paths.

8.1 Why not start with deep learning?
A deep sequence model such as an RNN, transformer, or neural recommender could be appropriate at 
a larger maturity stage, but it was not the right first production candidate for this problem. The goal 
was not to win an academic benchmark. The goal was to produce a system that stakeholders could 
trust, engineers could wire into the data warehouse, and commercial teams could act on.
 The dataset likely had sparse transitions for many products and protocol states.
 Business users needed explainability, not just a black-box score.
 Protocol constraints and practitioner context needed explicit modeling.
 A simple transparent baseline was easier to validate, replay, and roll back.
 The higher-order Markov and ranking layers could later be extended into personalized Markov 
chains or factorized sequential recommenders if volume justified it.
8.2 Why not treat all repeat purchases as reorders?
Because in protocol commerce, switching SKUs may be a sign of success. A direct reorder model alone 
would penalize customers who progress through intended steps. The correct design separated same-
SKU reorder from pathway continuation, then combined both in the ranking layer.

9. Evaluation and Validation Plan
The right evaluation for this model was temporal, not random. Random splits leak future customer 
behavior into training. The model should be trained on earlier orders and tested on later orders so the 
evaluation simulates actual recommendation timing.
9.1 Temporal validation design
8. Choose a historical cutoff date.
9. Train transition matrices, affinity rules, segments, and reorder models using orders before the 
cutoff.
10. Score customers at the cutoff using only their known history at that point.
11. Compare the top-k recommendations to actual purchases after the cutoff.
12. Evaluate separately by customer type, protocol cohort, product family, and first-time versus 
repeat customers.

9.3 Baselines
A credible ML project compares the model against simple baselines. For this use case, the required 
baselines were:
 Global popularity: recommend the most purchased products overall.
 Category popularity: recommend the most purchased products inside the prior product family.
 Same-SKU reorder: recommend the same product the customer last purchased.
 Practitioner/channel baseline: recommend top products by account type or channel.
 Protocol rule baseline: recommend the next manually defined protocol step.

14.2 Short case study copy
I designed an AI-assisted product prediction and recommendation system for a protocol-based 
wellness commerce environment. The company sold products through a mix of practitioner, partner, 
and direct consumer journeys, where customers often moved through multi-step product protocols 
rather than simply reordering the same SKU.
The business problem was that traditional ecommerce analytics could show repeat purchase rates, 
but could not explain sequence behavior. A customer who did not reorder the same product might 
still be progressing through a protocol. That distinction mattered for retention, product 
recommendations, practitioner support, bundling, and customer education.

I used GPT-4 as a technical acceleration tool to design the data pipeline, SQL transformations, Python 
modeling logic, validation plan, formulas, and documentation. The AI never received PII, raw 
customer records, credentials, or direct database access. I only supplied sanitized schemas, column 
names, table names, business rules, and synthetic examples so the generated logic could later be 
wired to the real datasets.
The final model architecture used a Markov chain as the core sequence engine, supported by higher-
order pathway modeling, reorder propensity scoring, product affinity analysis, behavioral 
segmentation, and a weighted recommendation ranking layer. The output was a system capable of 
estimating likely next products, same-SKU reorders, protocol continuation, and product relationships 
across different customer cohorts.
The key insight was that generic reorder probability was low for isolated purchases, but materially 
stronger when buyers were attached to a practitioner, bundle, protocol, or pathway. That shifted the 
commercial interpretation from 'customers are not reordering' to 'customers behave differently 
depending on where they are in the product journey.'
My contract ended before I could verify whether the models were deployed into production, so I 
describe the work as an offline validated model design and analytics handoff rather than a confirmed 
production deployment. The project demonstrates how AI can be used responsibly to accelerate 
enterprise analytics and ML delivery without exposing sensitive customer data.
